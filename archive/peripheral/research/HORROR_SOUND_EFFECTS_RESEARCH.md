## 2. What the science says about horror audio

### Sound drives the startle more than picture
Auditory stimuli are processed faster than visual; viewers cannot “look away” from sound the way they can from an image. Horror builds dread in the **buildup** (sound or its absence), then the **stinger** supplies much of the physical jump ([Slate, 2021](https://slate.com/culture/2021/10/how-jump-scares-work-and-how-to-tolerate-them.html) — summarizes startle literature).

### Four stages of a jump scare (audiovisual)
Recent experimental work models horror scenes as: **baseline → anticipation → arrival (stinger onset) → return to baseline**. Anticipatory cues prime viewers even when no scare follows; scares without anticipation feel cheaper but can still startle ([Frontiers in Psychology, 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1569394/full)).

### Acoustic startle events (ASEs) in horror marketing
Analysis of 173 startle events in US horror trailers found: many are **preceded by silence or quiet ground**; figure/ground contrast matters; startle is often **sound + cut + motion** together ([Brill / Redfern, acoustic startles in horror trailers](https://brill.com/view/journals/artp/11/3-4/article-p201_001.xml)).

### Silence is not “no audio”
“Silence” in film is controlled **room tone + removal of layers**. Dropping ambience before a hit makes the next event feel louder — dynamic contrast ([Kerins 2011, cited in Redfern thesis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/374805988); [The Conversation, 2024](https://theconversation.com/what-makes-a-film-score-frightening-expert-explains-the-techniques-that-build-tension-and-make-us-jump-216121)).

### Integrated soundtrack
Effective horror treats **dialogue, music, SFX, ambience, and silence** as one system — not a stack of loud layers ([Greene 2016; Kulezic-Wilson 2019; Segura 2022 — cited in Frontiers 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1569394/full)).

### Stinger craft
- **Fast attack** — volume must hit threshold in milliseconds, not a slow swell ([startle psychophysics — summarized in Curiouser Institute / Cat People bus scare analysis](https://medium.com/curiouserinstitute/the-eternal-jump-scare-79c6907a8c30)).
- **Short duration** — 0.1–0.3s burst + brief tail; long noise reads as “static,” not scare.
- **Dissonance / high register** — violin stabs, harsh transients mimic scream “roughness” ([The Conversation, 2024](https://theconversation.com/what-makes-a-film-score-frightening-expert-explains-the-techniques-that-build-tension-and-make-us-jump-216121)).

## 3. Diegetic vs non-diegetic — when each SFX type is useful

| Type | Characters hear it? | **Use when** | **Don't use when** |
|------|---------------------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Diegetic** (footsteps, door, cam beep, tap on speaker) | Yes | Escalation beats — grounds the wrong detail; mute viewers still read visually | Over every line of VO — masks hook |
| **Non-diegetic** (drone, riser, stinger, heartbeat bed) | No | Anticipation + false calm + finale | Whole video — becomes wallpaper |
| **Acousmatic** (source off-screen / unknown) | Unclear | “Something is in the house” beats — prediction error | Finale lunge — viewer needs visual sync |
| **Silence / room tone** | N/A | **False calm** (20–26s) — mandatory contrast before sting | Hook — need immediate unease |

*Craft framing: diegetic vs non-diegetic layering ([practitioner summary — diegetic/non-diegetic in horror](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0nGk8VCVCWw); aligns with film-sound scholarship in Redfern thesis).*

## 4. Don't Blink beat map → SFX decisions (~30s)

| Phase | Time | Audio job | Useful SFX | Skip |
|-------|------|-----------|------------|------|
| **Hook** | 0–3s | Impossible detail — instant wrongness | Single diegetic ping: cam motion alert, text whoosh, mirror tick | Risers, loud stingers |
| **Pattern** | 3–12s | Normal fractures | Soft room tone + one micro-cue per cut (refresh tap, lock click) | Continuous drone |
| **Escalation** | 12–20s | Each beat worse | Footstep/off-screen creak, IR cam static burst, muffled knock | Jump stings mid-video |
| **False calm** | 20–26s | **Safety signal** | Strip music/SFX to near-silence; optional 40Hz-feel drone at **-24dB** | Any loud transient |
| **Finale** | last ~2–3s | Earned startle | **Stinger** (fast attack) + optional diegetic slam/lunge thump synced to visual | Long white-noise (reads cheap) |
| **Suspense replay** | last 3s | Replay bait, no scare | Fade drone + hold room tone; **no stinger** | Jump sting |

This matches our visual beat map in `HORROR_PSYCHOLOGY_DEEP_RESEARCH.md` and owner policy: **finale scare only sometimes** (`suspense_replay` drafts).

## 5. SFX library categories (what to search in CapCut)

CapCut → **Audio → Sound effects** (also import WAV/MP3).

| Category | Examples | Beat |
|----------|----------|------|
| **UI / tech** | notification, cam shutter, glitch tick | Hook, security-cam pillar |
| **Foley / house** | floor creak, door handle, muffled knock | Escalation |
| **Body / breath** | inhale, heartbeat (subtle) | Pre-scare anticipation only |
| **Risers** | short low riser (1–2s) | Never on false calm — only pre-20s if needed |
| **Impacts / stingers** | cinematic hit, horror sting, reverse cymbal | **Finale only** (finale profile) |
| **Ambience** | wind, room hum, night interior | Under VO at -18 to -24dB |

**CapCut search terms:** `horror`, `suspense`, `cinematic hit`, `whoosh`, `glitch`, `heartbeat`, `door`, `footsteps`, `camera`.

## 6. Mixing rules (voice first)

1. **VO is king** — never let SFX compete with hook line or scare sentence ([CapCut audio course — voice first](https://cursa.app/en/page/audio-enhancements-in-capcut-clean-voice-music-balance-and-sound-effects)).
2. **Levels (starting points):** VO 0dB · diegetic SFX **-12 to -18dB** under speech · ambience **-20 to -28dB** · finale stinger **-6 to 0dB** (short peak only).
3. **Duck music** — if using YT Audio Library bed: auto-duck or 4-keyframe manual dip to 20–30% under VO ([CapCut ducking guide](https://videowizardtools.com/keyframes-in-capcut/)).
4. **Place SFX 1–3 frames before the cut** — brain anticipates the visual ([practitioner timing — align transient before peak](https://www.pippit.ai/blog/capcut-alternative/how-to-add-sound-effects-on-capcut)).
5. **Fade tails** — 2–4 frame fade-in/out on micro-cues; **no fade-in on stinger** (attack must be instant).
6. **Mute-safe** — every scare beat must read visually; SFX is reinforcement ([Jenny 05 + our pipeline](data/research/HORROR_PSYCHOLOGY_DEEP_RESEARCH.md)).

## 7. What fails (learned from draft #3 feedback)

| Mistake | Why it fails | Fix |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| Long white-noise “sting” | No fast attack; no visual sync | Short impact SFX in CapCut + visual flash at render |
| Same static layer whole video | No dynamic contrast | Strip layers at false calm |
| Mid-video jump stings | Breaks earned finale; confuses VO | SFX accents only; sting at end (finale profile) |
| SFX louder than VO | Mute viewers bounce | CapCut volume + ducking |
