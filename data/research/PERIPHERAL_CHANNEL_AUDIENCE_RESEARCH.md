# Peripheral — channel quality & target audience research

**Assessed:** 2026-06-12  
**Brand:** Peripheral (horror Shorts · faceless · ~30s · finale scare on most uploads)  
**Sources:** YouTube Shorts algorithm literature (2025–2026), audience-strategy frameworks (vidIQ, Studio Audience tab), in-repo horror psychology research, analog-horror Shorts competitor patterns, Peripheral brand positioning (`channel/brand/identity.md`).

---

## Executive summary (plain English)

A **good YouTube channel** is not “lots of random videos.” It is a **clear promise** delivered repeatedly to a **specific group of people** the algorithm can recognize and match.

For **Peripheral**, the winning move is:

1. **Pick an audience first** — late-night, alone, mute-first horror scrollers who want a **fast story + earned scare**, not gore or 20-minute essays.
2. **Package every Short for that person** — hook in 2s, new visual every 2–4s, captions burned in, 30–45s length, volume warning when there is a sting.
3. **Let data refine the audience** — after 5–10 uploads, Studio tells you age, country, and which videos held attention; narrow from there.
4. **Brand is the moat** — staring eye, Peripheral name, *don't blink* on merch/descriptions; anthology pillars rotate so it does not feel like one template.

**Audience before niche:** “Horror” is too broad. “People who watch analog/found-footage Shorts alone after dark on their phone” is ownable.

## Part 1 — What makes a *good* channel (not just viral once)

### 1.1 The algorithm’s real job

YouTube is a **matchmaker**, not a judge. It asks: *Who will watch this all the way through?* Then shows the video to that cluster.

A good channel makes that easy:

| Signal | What it means | Peripheral lever |
|--------|----------------|------------------|
| **Watch-through / retention** | Did strangers finish? | 30–40s scripts, false calm, finale sting; cut every 2–3s |
| **Topic cluster clarity** | Can YouTube bucket you? | Horror · analog · scary stories · faceless — consistent metadata |
| **Session depth** | Do they watch another? | Same brand, rotate scare pillar, pinned “what story next?” |
| **Upload consistency** | Enough tests to learn | 1 Short/day max (YPP); steady schedule same timezone |
| **Trust** | No bait-and-switch | Volume warning, synthetic disclosure, no misleading titles |

**2026 Shorts benchmarks (industry synthesis, not gospel):**

- **Length sweet spot:** ~30–45s (under 15s often fails absolute watch-time bars; over 60s drops on mobile).
- **Retention gate:** ~65%+ average view duration on sub-30s Shorts; ~50%+ on 30–60s to earn wider push.
- **Cold audience:** ~70%+ of Shorts views from **non-subscribers** — you are always pitching strangers.
- **Seed window:** First ~30–60 minutes after publish matter; post when your target timezone is awake (see §4).

A **good** channel hits retention repeatedly. A **lucky** channel hits once and cannot repeat.

### 1.2 What separates durable channels from template farms

**Good (durable)**

- One **viewer promise** per video (“30s story, something’s wrong, watch the end”).
- **Variation inside a frame** — same mood, different hook pillar (reflection, sound, cam, time, text).
- **Brand recognition** — eye logo, name, tone; subscribers know what they’re getting.
- **Feedback loop** — retention graphs → next script hooks.
- **Honest packaging** — title = first line of story; scare not hidden in clickbait.

**Bad (YPP / audience risk)**

- Same visual grammar every upload with only text changed (spam signal).
- Batch QA uploads, build suffix titles, duplicate hooks.
- Gore, shock without setup, fake emergency alerts.
- Chasing trends unrelated to core audience (breaks cluster).

Peripheral should feel like **a label** (clothing + eye), not a **single trick**.

### 1.3 The five pillars of channel quality

1. **Positioning** — Who is this for? What moment are they in?  
2. **Packaging** — Title, first frame, first line of VO (hook).  
3. **Product** — Script pacing, visuals, captions, audio sting.  
4. **Distribution** — Post time, SEO tags, Shorts vs long-form strategy.  
5. **Learning** — Analytics → next topics → avoid rules in Codex.

Automation (your north star) only works when 1–5 are **codified** — which this doc supports.

## Part 2 — Audience vs niche (how to think)

### 2.1 Definitions

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Niche** | Topic category on YouTube | Horror Shorts, analog horror |
| **Audience** | People with shared **context, mood, and habit** | Alone at night, phone, wants quick dread |
| **Persona** | One fictional “ideal viewer” | See §4.1 |
| **First audience** | Early subscribers + seed pool YouTube tests with | Who already watched Video #1 |

**Rule:** Pick **audience** first. Niche follows.

Wrong: “We make horror.”  
Right: “We make 30s faceless scares for people scrolling alone after 9pm who finish videos with a twist at the end.”

### 2.2 How to pick a target audience (decision framework)

Use this order — do not skip steps.

#### Step A — List 3–5 *candidate* audiences (hypotheses)

For Peripheral, strong candidates:

| ID | Candidate audience | Why they might fit |
|----|-------------------|-------------------|
| **A** | Late-night mobile scrollers (18–34) | Shorts peak evening; horror performs after dark |
| **B** | Analog / found-footage horror fans | CCTV, grain, liminal — matches production lane |
| **C** | Creepypasta / story-time listeners | Faceless VO + story; mute captions matter |
| **D** | Jumpscare / thrill seekers | Finale sting; volume warning filters wrong viewers |
| **E** | General “scary Shorts” binge watchers | Broadest; hardest to own |

#### Step B — Score each candidate (1–5)

| Criterion | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Size** | Are enough people searching/watching this? (competitor sub counts, search suggest) |
| **Reachability** | Can Shorts algorithm find them without you being famous? |
| **Fit** | Can Peripheral produce for them weekly without new gear? |
| **Monetization** | YPP, merch (hoodies), future Patreon? |
| **Differentiation** | Can you stand out vs 100 other horror channels? |
| **Retention match** | Do they finish 30s stories? |

**Recommended primary:** **A + B** blended — “late-night analog-horror Shorts scrollers.”  
**Secondary:** C (story listeners).  
**Deprioritize:** D alone (attracts click-off if scare weak); E alone (too broad).

#### Step C — Validate with evidence (not vibes)

| Stage | Channel size | What to use |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| **0–100 subs** | You are here | Competitor comment mining, search autocomplete, outlier videos in niche |
| **100–1K** | Next | Studio → Audience → Demographics + “Channels they watch” |
| **1K+** | Later | Retention by video, A/B hooks, polls in comments |

**Competitor research (no views needed):**

1. Find 5 channels: analog horror, scary stories Shorts, faceless narration (not necessarily huge).
2. Read **top comments** on their best Shorts: “I jumped,” “watch till the end,” “part 2?” = your language.
3. Note **hook patterns** that repeat — impossible detail, alone at home, wrong reflection.
4. Use **outlier tool** (vidIQ etc.) for videos 5–10× channel average — copy structure, not story.

#### Step D — Write one persona (mandatory)

If you cannot describe one person, the algorithm cannot either.

See §4.1 — **Jordan**.

#### Step E — Commit for 10 uploads, then refine

Do not change audience every video. Give YouTube **10 consistent signals**, then read data.

**Change audience when:** retention flatlines *and* comments are from wrong crowd (“fake,” “not scary,” kids).  
**Do not change when:** one video flops — flukes happen in seed pool.

### 2.3 “First audience” strategy

Early subscribers and anyone who watched Video #1 (mirror) are your **seed**. YouTube tests new Shorts on people similar to them.

**Implication:** Next uploads should feel like **the same person would swipe yes** — same tone, length, faceless, earned scare — even when pillar rotates.

Do not pivot to comedy horror or 60s lore essays yet; you’d confuse the seed.

## Part 3 — Target audience for Peripheral (recommended)

### 3.1 Primary audience statement

> **English-speaking mobile viewers, roughly 18–34, watching alone in the evening (8pm–1am local), who want a 30-second uncanny story with a clear hook and a payoff at the end — and who often watch on mute first.**

### 3.2 Psychographics (more important than age)

| Trait | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Mood** | Open to dread, not seeking comfort content |
| **Setting** | Bed, couch, dark room — “alone tonight” matches VO |
| **Behavior** | Binge 5–20 Shorts; decides in **2 seconds** |
| **Expectation** | Story implied, not explained; scare earned not random |
| **Brand affinity** | Cool-minimal horror (Peripheral hoodies), not meme shitpost |
| **Friction** | Hates loud jumps without warning — **volume warning helps** |

### 3.3 Demographics (hypothesis until Studio confirms)

| Field | Hypothesis | After 5+ uploads |
|-------|------------|------------------|
| Age | 18–34 core | Studio Audience tab |
| Gender | Slight male skew common in horror Shorts | Confirm |
| Geo | US, UK, Canada, Australia first | Post in US Eastern evening |
| Device | 80%+ mobile | Vertical 9:16, captions mandatory |
| Language | English | Future: localized captions optional |

### 3.4 What they subscribe *for*

Not “horror” generically. They subscribe for:

- **Reliable payoff** — end is worth the wait  
- **Specific wrongness** — timestamp, reflection, knock, cam — **rotated**  
- **Aesthetic** — cold, faceless, analog when relevant  
- **Identity** — “I watch Peripheral” (merch later)

### 3.5 Who is NOT the audience (say no on purpose)

- Kids / family co-viewing (tone, volume, YPP)
- Gore / torture fans (we imply, not splatter)
- Long-form horror essay watchers (different format)
- People who want creator personality vlogs
- “AI is evil” commenters — disclose synthetic, don’t debate in titles

Narrowing **out** is how you stop feeling templated to everyone.

## Part 4 — Persona & packaging

### 4.1 Persona: Jordan (working name)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Age | 24 |
| Location | US, apartment, Eastern time |
| When they watch | 10:30pm–12:30am, in bed, headphones optional |
| Other channels | Analog horror compilations, scary story Shorts, liminal spaces |
| Why they click | First line is **wrong** — “reflection blinked late,” “cam flagged motion” |
| Why they finish | False calm tricked them; sting got them; they comment “I jumped” |
| Why they subscribe | Predictable **quality** + variable **story** |
| Merch | Would wear black hoodie with white line eye if it looks like fashion not costume |

**Content test:** Would Jordan share this Short to a friend with “watch the end”? If no, fix hook or pacing.

### 4.2 Packaging checklist (every upload)

- [ ] Title = hook keyword, first 40 chars (no build suffix on public)
- [ ] First spoken line matches title energy in **3 seconds**
- [ ] Captions on, safe zone, mute-readable
- [ ] New visual or angle every **2–4 seconds**
- [ ] Description: Peripheral + don't blink + 3–5 hashtags
- [ ] Synthetic media disclosed
- [ ] Post time: Wed–Fri, **3–5pm US Eastern** (seed when US awake) OR **9–11pm** for horror mood test — **A/B after 10 videos**

### 4.3 Viewer journey (not random uploads)

Think **playlist psychology**:

1. **Hook variety** — rotate scare pillar  
2. **Same contract** — ~30s, faceless, Peripheral brand  
3. **Binge path** — “one more” → second Short auto-plays  
4. **Comment CTA** — “What story next?” trains engaged commenters  
5. **Merch** — eye hoodie for superfans after ~10K subs (not day one)

## Part 5 — How Peripheral wins vs “templated” feel

You rejected universe-locked names because they imply **same mechanic every time**.

| Templated (avoid) | Peripheral (do) |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Same CCTV UI every video | Rotate **pillar**; analog when it serves the story |
| Same monster | Faceless threat; different mask per story |
| Same title shape | Hook line varies; brand in description |
| Lore exposition | One-night story; no bible required |
| Brand = plot device | Brand = **feeling watched** + eye mark |

**Anthology + label** = Nike drops different designs, same swoosh.

## Part 6 — Picking the audience: quick decision tree

```
Start
  │
  ├─ Do you have 10+ uploads with analytics?
  │     YES → Studio Audience tab → double down on top retention demo
  │     NO  → Continue below
  │
  ├─ Who do competitor COMMENTS describe? (mine 5 channels)
  │
  ├─ Can you serve them 3x/week without burnout?
  │     NO  → narrow audience
  │     YES → Write persona (Jordan)
  │
  ├─ Does your hook work in 2s for them?
  │     NO  → fix scripts, not audience
  │     YES → Lock audience 10 uploads
  │
  └─ Retention < 60% at scale?
        YES → adjust pacing/captions before changing audience
        NO  → expand secondary audience (e.g. story-time fans)
```

## Part 7 — Metrics that matter (ignore vanity)

| Metric | Why |
|--------|-----|
| **Average view duration %** | Shorts distribution gate |
| **Retention at 3s** | Hook worked? |
| **Retention at 20s** | False calm / middle held? |
| **Retention last 3s** | Finale worth it? |
| **Comment quality** | “I jumped” > “nice video” |
| **Subscriber conversion per 1K views** | Brand sticking? |
| **RPM** | Later — after YPP stable |

Views without retention = algorithm stops testing.
