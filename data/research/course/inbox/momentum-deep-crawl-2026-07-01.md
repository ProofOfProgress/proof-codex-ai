A cheaper image-to-video workflow using the prompt builder and Kling.

Another strategy to use that is cheaper on credits, once you find your product:

1
Get the stock image from the TikTok listing. (If you don’t have a stock image, take any image from the listing on TikTok Shop, drop it into a normal ChatGPT chat, and ask it to put it on a plain white background.)
2
Drop that image into the prompt builder and it will automatically generate you a prompt: Product Video Prompt Builder. You can also send it to the AI Video Director™ agent to generate your prompt.
3
Take the prompt and the same image and upload to KLING-AI OMNI Video-01: kling.ai/app/omni

Optional extra step for higher quality: take the stock image from step one into a normal ChatGPT chat and ask it to generate an image of that product in an appropriate scene (a kitchen, a backyard, etc). STRATEGY 03
The Dakota™ Workflow

A full written SOP. Build a master image script, then a video script, and lock them in.

This is a written workflow, not a video. It gives you two reusable scripts: one that drops any product into a clean lifestyle photo, and one that animates that photo into a smooth UGC clip. You build each script once with Claude, test it, then lock it as your master template for every product.

Part 1 — The Image Script (Nano Banana Pro)

Have these three things open before you start: a product reference image (clearer is better), your image tool (Nano Banana Pro or ChatGPT image), and Claude.

Phase 1 — Have Claude write your Nano Banana script

The script Claude writes is what you paste into Nano Banana Pro. You never write it yourself. Paste this into Claude:

Copy
You are a 180-IQ product scriptwriter, and your goal is to create the image that becomes a high-converting TikTok Shop affiliate video. Write me a reusable image-generation script for Nano Banana Pro. The job: take a product reference image and place that exact product into a realistic, aspirational-but-attainable home, as a vertical 9:16 photorealistic lifestyle photo. It must work for ANY product and defend against every common failure. Make sure it covers: the product's exact shape, parts, and part count; locking the exact color; building a brand-new environment instead of copying the reference background; placing the product where it'd really be used; correct real-world scale; gravity and how the product stands; bundles and multipacks showing every piece; framing that leaves room on both sides for a later camera pan; realistic materials and lighting that looks good but never unrealistic; and a clear list of things to never include (mirrors, fire, moving water, people, other brands). Write it as one continuous set of rules I can paste straight into Nano Banana Pro. Nano Banana Pro has no character limit, so make the script as detailed and complete as it needs to be to get the product perfect. Don't cut anything important, just keep every line a clear rule rather than filler.
The output Claude hands back, not this prompt, is what goes into Nano Banana Pro.

Then ask for three variations. Reply:

Copy
Now give me three different versions of that script. Same goal and rules, but vary the wording, the order, and how strongly each emphasizes product accuracy vs. environment. Label them Version 1, 2, and 3.

Phase 2 — Test all three

Pick one test product. Generate from all three versions using the same product image, 2 to 3 images per version so you are not judging off one lucky or unlucky result. Then score each image against this checklist.

✅
Image Score Checklist
•
Exact product, right number of parts, nothing added or missing
•
Color is a true match to the reference
•
Background is a NEW environment, not the reference’s
•
The setting makes sense for this product
•
Scale is believable (not too big or small for the room)
•
Open space on both sides (room to pan later)
•
Looks real, not CGI or fake-perfect
•
Zero banned items (mirrors, fire, water, people, other brands)

Write down what broke. Note which version scored best and list the specific failures, like "Version 2 changed the color" or "Version 1 added a lid that isn’t on the product."

On backgrounds: the best environment is a simple, realistic space where the buyer can already picture the product as if it were theirs. Sometimes that fits the product category (a blender on a kitchen counter, a dumbbell in a home gym), other times it is just a clean, elevated space. Either way keep it simple, believable, and never busy or cluttered.

STRATEGY
The Vault™

Our library of the highest-converting text-on-screen and caption strategies.

There are a lot of different text-on-screen (caption-on-screen) strategies people use. This is where we collect the ones that actually move the needle.

⛔
Rule #1 — Non-negotiable
•
The product name has to be showing on the video at all times. No exceptions.

Past that, there is no single right answer. A ton of caption styles work. When you pick one, judge it on two things:

🎯
How to pick your caption
•
Click-through and conversions. Does this caption actually make people tap and buy?
•
Your risk tolerance. How close to the line are you willing to run?

Example: putting the exact discount percentage on screen, or claims like "triple discount," can convert well, but they are riskier right now and cause a lot of violations. Some people get away with it, plenty do not. Higher reward, higher risk. Pick the style that matches your own tolerance, not someone else’s.

Universal captions (super safe). These convert well and stay low-risk. Swap in your product name and post. Remember, keep the product name on screen the whole time.

Copy
I am so sorry if you already grabbed an [insert product name], because the discount is huge today.
Copy
Before you ask...No it's not a typo. Yes the [insert product name] is literal Pennie's today..😱😅
Copy
This is your sign to finally grab the [insert product name].
Copy
Do NOT scroll past the [insert product name] if it has been sitting in your cart.
Copy
POV: you found the [insert product name] before everyone else did.
Copy
If you have been waiting on the [insert product name], now is the time.
Copy
Run, do not walk, to grab the [insert product name].
Copy
The [insert product name] everyone has been asking me about is finally back.
Copy
Stop overthinking the [insert product name] and just tap the cart.
Copy
Me telling you to grab the [insert product name] before it sells out again.
Copy
Your future self will thank you for grabbing the [insert product name] today.
Copy
Adding the [insert product name] to my cart before I change my mind.
Copy
The [insert product name] is about to be everywhere. Get it first.
Copy

Copy
Consider this your reminder to grab the [insert product name] you keep eyeing.
Copy
Trust me, you want the [insert product name] in your cart today.
Higher risk, higher reward. Everything below converts better but is riskier to run right now. These are the ones that get accounts violations. Only use them if you understand the trade-off and are okay taking the occasional hit. Product name still stays on screen the whole time.

1. The triple discount. Call out a stacked or "triple" discount on the product. It pulls a strong click-through because it reads as a rare deal, but this style of wording is one of the most flagged right now. On-screen text example:

Copy
Triple discount on the [insert product name] today only

2. Crossed-out percentage. Show a small, crossed-out anchor discount, then the real discount right next to it in bigger, red text. The contrast makes the deal feel massive. Layout:

🔻
On-screen layout
•
Small and crossed out: the anchor number, like 10% OFF
•
Big and in red right after it: the real number, like 60% OFF
•
Product name stays visible the whole time

3. Huge sale + product name. Keep it dead simple. Big "HUGE DEAL" text up top, then a quick urgency line, then the product name right under it. Low effort and reads clean, but it still leans on sale language so it carries some risk. Example:

Copy
HUGE DEAL 🔥
ACT FAST ⏳
[insert product name]

Supplement Product(s)

Prompts for supplements, with and without amenities.

This is broken down by different types of supplements. A generic version of the prompt for simpler images is provided first before involving amenities such as pills or gummies.

The following is assumed:
- 1 Reference Image of the product (Image 1 | Most Left Image)
- 1 Isolated Product Image of the product (Image 2 | Most Right or Middle Image)
- 1 Isolated Amenity Image from the product if applicable (Image 3 | Most Left Image)

For multiple of a product, put this sentence last / after the second sentence: “Put X of the product from image 2 side by side.” (Recommended to only do 2 side by side, unless it is a trio or larger bundle, to maintain good text on product.)

Supplement Product | No Amenities Involved

Copy
Only use image 1 as a reference for the scale and dimensions of the product from image 2. Put the product from image 2 on a cream colored marble kitchen table with natural lighting. (photo realistic, matching lighting)

Copy
Only use image 1 as a reference for the scale and dimensions of the products from images 2 - 3. Put the product from image 2 on a cream colored marble kitchen table with natural lighting. Put 3 of the product from image 3 scattered around the product from image 2. The product from image 3 is 1/4th of an inch in size. (photo realistic, matching lighting)
